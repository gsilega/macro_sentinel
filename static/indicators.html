Indicator Reference

What each indicator measures, why the Fed watches it, and what the readings mean for rate decisions. Use this before FOMC meetings.

Inflation
CPI — Consumer Price Index
Monthly | Bureau of Labor Statistics CPIAUCSL
Lagging
What it measures

The average price change of a basket of ~80,000 goods and services bought by urban US consumers — food, housing, gas, healthcare, apparel, and more. Reported as year-over-year % change.

Why the Fed watches it

CPI is the headline number that markets and media react to most strongly. While the Fed targets PCE, a surprise CPI print moves markets instantly and shapes public inflation expectations — which then feed back into actual inflation.

🔴 Above 3%: Inflation running hot — rate cuts unlikely
🟢 Below 2%: Inflation at or below target — rate cuts on the table
Core CPI — Excluding Food & Energy
Monthly | Bureau of Labor Statistics CPILFESL
Lagging
What it measures

Same as CPI but with food and energy prices stripped out. Food and energy are volatile and driven by weather, wars, and supply shocks — things monetary policy can't fix. Core CPI isolates the "sticky" underlying inflation trend.

Why the Fed watches it

Core CPI is a cleaner signal than headline CPI. The Fed focuses heavily on "supercore" (services ex-housing) within this, because services inflation is driven by wages — which monetary policy can actually influence.

🔴 Above 3%: Sticky inflation — very unlikely to cut
🟢 Below 2.5%: Trend in the right direction — opens door to cuts
PCE — Personal Consumption Expenditures
Monthly | Bureau of Economic Analysis PCEPI
Lagging Fed's Target
What it measures

Inflation in spending by US households and non-profits, drawn from GDP accounts. Broader than CPI: covers what consumers actually spend (not a fixed basket), and adjusts when people substitute cheaper alternatives.

Why the Fed watches it

The Fed's 2% inflation target is PCE, not CPI. PCE typically runs about 0.2–0.4% below CPI. When Powell says "we're targeting 2% inflation," he means PCE. This is THE number that directly governs rate decisions.

🔴 Above 2.5%: Above target — Fed will hold or hike
🟢 At or below 2%: At target — rate cuts justified
PPI — Producer Price Index
Monthly | Bureau of Labor Statistics PPIFID
Leading
What it measures

Prices received by domestic producers for their output — goods and services before they reach consumers. Measures cost pressures at the wholesale/manufacturing stage of the supply chain.

Why the Fed watches it

PPI leads CPI by roughly 1–3 months. When producers pay more for inputs, they eventually pass costs to consumers. A rising PPI warns of future CPI increases before they happen, giving the Fed a forward-looking inflation signal.

🔴 Rising PPI: Consumer price increases coming in 1–3 months
🟢 Falling PPI: Input cost relief, likely to slow consumer inflation
Labor Market
NFP — Non-Farm Payrolls
Monthly | Bureau of Labor Statistics PAYEMS
Lagging
What it measures

Total number of paid workers in the US economy, excluding farm employees (too seasonal to compare month-over-month), government, private household, and non-profit employees. The headline monthly jobs number.

Why the Fed watches it

The Fed has a "dual mandate": price stability AND maximum employment. Strong payrolls signal a tight labor market, which drives wage growth, which drives services inflation. Weak payrolls give the Fed cover to cut rates.

🔴 Above 200K: Labor market too strong — rate cuts pushed back
🟢 Below 100K: Labor market cooling — strengthens case for cuts
Unemployment Rate (U6) — Broad Unemployment
Monthly | Bureau of Labor Statistics U6RATE
Lagging
What it measures

The "real" unemployment rate. U3 (headline) counts people without jobs who are actively looking. U6 adds: discouraged workers (gave up looking) + people working part-time who want full-time work. Better captures true labor market slack.

Why the Fed watches it

U6 shows how much room exists before the labor market tightens further. A low U6 means workers have real bargaining power → wages rise → services inflation persists. A rising U6 signals weakening demand and justifies rate relief.

🔴 U6 below 7%: Very tight market — wage pressure likely persisting
🟢 U6 above 8%: Meaningful slack — labor conditions supportive of cuts
JOLTS — Job Openings
Monthly (lagged ~6 weeks) | Bureau of Labor Statistics JTSJOL
Leading
What it measures

Total number of unfilled job positions across US employers on the last business day of each month. A direct measure of unmet labor demand — how urgently businesses want to hire.

Why the Fed watches it

When openings are high relative to unemployed workers, employers compete for talent → wages rise → services inflation increases. Jerome Powell famously cited the openings-to-unemployed ratio as a key measure of labor market tightness.

🔴 Above 9M: Extremely tight — strong wage pressure, rate cuts unlikely
🟢 Below 7M: Openings normalizing — labor market less inflationary
Average Hourly Earnings
Monthly | Bureau of Labor Statistics CES0500000003
Coincident
What it measures

Average hourly wages for all private nonfarm employees. The absolute level matters for cost-of-living, but the Fed focuses on year-over-year % change as a measure of wage inflation pressure.

Why the Fed watches it

Wages are the dominant input cost in services industries (restaurants, healthcare, education). Services inflation is the stickiest kind — once wages go up, they rarely come back down, and businesses price accordingly. Wage growth above ~3.5% is incompatible with 2% services inflation.

🔴 YoY above 4%: Wage-driven inflation risk — Fed unlikely to cut
🟢 YoY around 3–3.5%: Consistent with 2% inflation target
Economic Growth
GDP — Real Gross Domestic Product
Quarterly | Bureau of Economic Analysis GDPC1
Lagging
What it measures

The total inflation-adjusted value of all goods and services produced in the US in a quarter, reported as Seasonally Adjusted Annual Rate (SAAR). The broadest single measure of economic output and health.

Why the Fed watches it

Strong GDP gives the Fed room to hold rates high without tipping into recession. Weak or negative GDP shifts the calculus: holding rates high risks recession, and the Fed must weigh the cost of its medicine against inflation progress.

🔴 Above 3%: Economy running hot — can absorb high rates
🟢 Below 1% or negative: Recession risk — strong case for rate relief
ISM PMI — Manufacturing Purchasing Managers Index
Monthly | Institute for Supply Management NAPM
Leading
What it measures

A survey of purchasing managers at ~400 manufacturing firms asking about new orders, production, employment, deliveries, and inventories. A diffusion index: above 50 = more companies expanding than contracting. Below 50 = contraction.

Why the Fed watches it

PMI is released the first business day of the month (before most other data). Purchasing managers make forward-looking buying decisions — their collective behavior predicts production, hiring, and growth 1–3 months ahead.

🔴 Above 55: Strong expansion — adding fuel to an already hot economy
🟢 Below 47: Contraction — rising recession risk, dovish pivot likely
Retail Sales
Monthly | Census Bureau RSXFS
Coincident
What it measures

Total monthly sales at retail stores and food service establishments, excluding autos and gas (to reduce volatility). Directly measures consumer spending patterns — the largest driver of US economic output.

Why the Fed watches it

Consumer spending is ~70% of US GDP. When retail sales are strong, demand is pulling through the economy — creating both growth and inflationary pressure. A sharp drop in retail sales signals demand destruction, which could justify rate cuts to stimulate.

🔴 Accelerating MoM growth: Demand-driven inflation risk
🟢 Declining or negative MoM: Consumer pulling back — rate cut supportive
Market Rates & Expectations
2-Year Treasury Yield
Daily | US Treasury / FRED DGS2
Leading
What it measures

The annualized return on 2-year US government bonds. Because these bonds mature in 2 years, investors price their expectations of where interest rates will be over that period directly into the yield. It's the bond market's forecast of Fed policy.

Why the Fed watches it

The 2Y yield is the most Fed-sensitive bond in the market. Unlike 10Y or 30Y yields (which also price in growth and term premium), the 2Y is almost entirely about near-term rate expectations. When the 2Y falls sharply, the market is pricing in imminent rate cuts — a signal the Fed takes seriously.

🔴 Above 4.5%: Market expects rates to stay high — hawkish regime
🟢 Below 3.5%: Market pricing in significant rate cuts ahead
Fed Funds Rate (Effective)
Daily | Federal Reserve EFFR
Coincident
What it measures

The actual overnight interest rate at which US banks lend reserves to each other. This is the rate the FOMC directly sets via its target range. The "effective" rate is the volume-weighted average of actual overnight transactions.

Why the Fed watches it

This IS the policy rate — the primary instrument the Fed uses to influence all other interest rates in the economy. Mortgages, auto loans, credit cards, business borrowing, and the 2Y Treasury all respond to where this rate sits. It's the benchmark for all other context in this dashboard.

🔴 Above 4.5%: Restrictive monetary policy — economy under rate pressure
🟢 Below 3%: Accommodative — stimulating growth and risk-taking
